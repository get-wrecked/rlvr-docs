# Fact Verification

## Overview

Fact verification tasks require the model to determine whether a natural language claim is supported, refuted, or left unresolved by a given body of evidence. The model must reason over evidence passages and produce a veracity label.

This domain sits at the intersection of natural language inference and knowledge retrieval. It is well-suited for RLVR because the gold labels are derived from knowledge bases or human annotation, and verification is a simple label comparison.

## Verification Mechanism

**Verification type: Exact**

The verification function compares the model's predicted label against the gold label. Standard label sets:

- **FEVER**: SUPPORTS, REFUTES, NOT ENOUGH INFO (3-way classification)
- **TabFact**: ENTAILED, REFUTED (2-way classification over tabular evidence)
- **HoVer**: SUPPORTED, NOT SUPPORTED (2-way, multi-hop)

For RLVR binary reward:
- `reward = 1` if predicted label matches gold label
- `reward = 0` otherwise

Some formulations also require the model to identify the relevant evidence sentences. In this case, verification checks both the label and the evidence selection (exact match on sentence IDs).

The verification is fully programmatic with no approximation: labels are discrete, and matching is exact.

## Dataset Sources

| Dataset | Size | Evidence Type | Labels | Notes |
|---------|------|---------------|--------|-------|
| FEVER | 185K claims | Wikipedia passages | SUPPORTS / REFUTES / NEI | Claims generated by annotators mutating Wikipedia sentences |
| TabFact | 118K claims | Wikipedia tables | ENTAILED / REFUTED | Requires numerical and logical reasoning over tables |
| HoVer | 26K claims | Wikipedia passages | SUPPORTED / NOT SUPPORTED | Multi-hop; requires reasoning over 2-4 evidence passages |
| VitaminC | 450K claims | Wikipedia revisions | SUPPORTS / REFUTES / NEI | Contrastive claim pairs from Wikipedia edits |
| CLIMATE-FEVER | 1.5K claims | Wikipedia | SUPPORTS / REFUTES / NEI / DISPUTED | Climate-specific claims |
| SciFact | 1.4K claims | Scientific abstracts | SUPPORTS / REFUTES / NEI | Scientific domain |
| MultiFC | 34K claims | News articles | Multiple veracity levels | Real-world fact-checking datasets |

**Scalability**: FEVER-style data can be generated at scale by mutating sentences from any knowledge source (Wikipedia, textbooks, news). Synthetic claim generation from Wikidata triples is also viable.

## Task Format

**Input**: A claim and (optionally) a set of evidence passages.

**Output**: A veracity label (SUPPORTS/REFUTES/NEI).

**Prompt template example**:
```
Given the following evidence, determine if the claim is SUPPORTED, REFUTED, or if there is NOT ENOUGH INFO.

Evidence: {evidence_passages}

Claim: {claim}

Verdict:
```

For retrieval-augmented variants, the model must first retrieve relevant evidence from a corpus, then classify.

## Difficulty Curriculum

1. **Level 1 — Simple entity claims**: "Barack Obama was born in Hawaii." Evidence directly states the fact. Single-hop.
2. **Level 2 — Negation and paraphrase**: Claims that rephrase evidence or include negation. Requires paraphrase detection.
3. **Level 3 — Numerical reasoning**: "The population of France exceeds 60 million." Requires comparing numbers from evidence.
4. **Level 4 — Multi-hop reasoning**: Claims requiring synthesis of 2+ evidence passages. "The director of Titanic also directed Avatar." (HoVer-style)
5. **Level 5 — Tabular evidence**: Claims about table data requiring aggregation, comparison, or counting (TabFact-style).
6. **Level 6 — Temporal and conditional claims**: "As of 2020, X was the largest..." Requires temporal scoping and conditional reasoning.
7. **Level 7 — Adversarial claims**: Claims designed to fool models, with subtle mutations that flip veracity (VitaminC-style).

## Limitations & Risks

- **NOT ENOUGH INFO is hard**: The NEI class is inherently ambiguous. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, and models struggle to distinguish "not stated" from "refuted."
- **Evidence quality**: When the model must retrieve its own evidence, verification of the label alone does not guarantee the model used correct reasoning. It may get the right answer for the wrong reasons.
- **Knowledge cutoff**: Claims about recent events may conflict with the model's training data, creating confusing reward signals.
- **Claim generation bias**: Synthetic claims may not reflect the distribution of real-world fact-checking needs.
- **Annotation artifacts**: FEVER has known annotation artifacts that allow models to predict labels from claims alone without reading evidence.
